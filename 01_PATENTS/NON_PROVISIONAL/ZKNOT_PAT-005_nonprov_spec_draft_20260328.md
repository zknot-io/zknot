## BACKGROUND OF THE INVENTION

### Field of the Invention

The present invention relates to hardware supply chain security, device lifecycle management, and cryptographic attestation. More particularly, the invention relates to systems and methods for recording device manufacturing and lifecycle events in a cryptographically verifiable, vendor-irrevocable manner — such that the customer holds permanent proof of device origin and configuration that the vendor cannot retroactively alter or deny.

### Description of Related Art

Hardware supply chain attacks, counterfeit devices, and undisclosed manufacturing modifications represent growing threats to organizations deploying hardware security devices. Current supply chain assurance approaches rely on vendor-controlled records: certificates of authenticity, chain-of-custody documentation, and tamper-evident packaging. These approaches share a fundamental architectural flaw: they are controlled by the vendor.

A vendor who wishes to conceal a manufacturing modification, deny a configuration error, or retroactively alter records can do so because the record-keeping infrastructure is vendor-owned. Certificate-based approaches have the same limitation: a vendor who issues a device certificate controls the ability to revoke, reissue, or modify certificate infrastructure, potentially altering the apparent history of any device.

This vendor control creates a trust dependency that is particularly problematic in government procurement, law enforcement hardware, and regulated industries where device provenance is subject to legal scrutiny. A federal agency procuring cryptographic attestation hardware cannot rely on vendor-held manufacturing records as independent evidence of device provenance when the vendor controls those records.

What is needed is a system in which manufacturing and lifecycle events are recorded in a manner that transfers permanent, tamper-evident, independently verifiable custody of that record to the customer at the moment of device receipt — such that no subsequent vendor action can alter, revoke, or deny what the customer received.

---

## SUMMARY OF THE INVENTION

The present invention provides a vendor-irrevocable device attestation system in which manufacturing and lifecycle events are recorded as signed, hash-chained ledger entries, stored in a device-resident ledger, and transferred to the customer at receipt. The customer executes a receipt attestation that commits to the entire prior vendor-signed ledger, after which the vendor cannot alter any manufacturing record without creating a detectable mismatch with the customer's independently held signed record.

## DETAILED DESCRIPTION OF THE INVENTION

### 1. Manufacturing Event Ledger

**1.1 Event Record Structure**

Each ledger entry comprises:
- `event_id`: sequential identifier
- `event_type`: enumeration — KEY_GENERATION | FIRMWARE_LOAD | CALIBRATION | INSPECTION | PACKAGING | SHIPMENT | RECEIPT | FIELD_EVENT
- `event_data`: structured data specific to event type
- `device_serial`: SE hardware serial number
- `timestamp_utc`: timestamp of event
- `signing_party`: identifier of signing party (vendor | customer | third party)
- `prior_entry_hash`: SHA-256 hash of the preceding entry
- `entry_hash`: SHA-256 hash of all above fields
- `signature`: ECDSA signature by the signing party over `entry_hash`

**1.2 Genesis Entry — Device Key Generation**

The first ledger entry is the KEY_GENERATION event, signed by the device's hardware secure element (HSE) itself — not by the vendor. This establishes, via the device's own signature, that the cryptographic key pair was generated on-device and was not imported from an external source. The genesis entry hash anchors the entire subsequent chain and is verifiable against the device's public key without any vendor involvement.

This is the root of trust: the customer can verify independently that the key was generated by the specific HSE identified by its hardware serial number, before any vendor manufacturing step occurred.

**1.3 Vendor-Signed Manufacturing Entries**

Following the genesis entry, the vendor signs entries for each manufacturing step:
- `FIRMWARE_LOAD`: firmware version, build hash, load timestamp, build environment identifier
- `CALIBRATION`: calibration parameters, test results, calibration equipment serial number, pass/fail result
- `INSPECTION`: inspection result, inspector identifier, inspection timestamp, inspection scope
- `PACKAGING`: packaging configuration, tamper seal serial number applied, packaging timestamp

Each vendor-signed entry includes `prior_entry_hash`, creating a hash chain. Insertion or deletion of any entry breaks the chain and is immediately detectable by hash recomputation.

**1.4 Shipment Entry**

Before shipment, the vendor signs a SHIPMENT entry containing: customer identifier, carrier and tracking information, shipment timestamp, and SHA-256 hash of the complete ledger at time of shipment. This entry records what the vendor attests was in the device at the moment it left vendor custody.

### 2. Customer Receipt and Vendor Irrevocability

**2.1 Receipt Event**

When the customer receives the device, the customer verification tool performs the following sequence:

Step 1: Reads the complete device-resident ledger from the device HSE or associated non-volatile storage.

Step 2: Verifies all vendor signatures against the vendor's registered public key.

Step 3: Verifies hash chain continuity from genesis through the SHIPMENT entry — no gaps, no broken links.

Step 4: Creates a RECEIPT entry signed by the customer:
- `customer_identifier`: customer organization identifier or public key fingerprint
- `receipt_timestamp`: time of receipt verification
- `verified_chain_hash`: SHA-256 hash of the complete ledger through the SHIPMENT entry
- `customer_signature`: ECDSA signature over all RECEIPT entry fields using a customer-held private key

Step 5: Appends the RECEIPT entry to the device-resident ledger.

Step 6: Stores a complete signed copy of the ledger (through RECEIPT) in the customer's own independent secure storage, separate from the device.

**2.2 Vendor Irrevocability Mechanism**

After customer receipt, vendor irrevocability is guaranteed by the following mechanism:

The customer holds a signed copy of all vendor-signed entries. The customer's RECEIPT entry contains `verified_chain_hash`, which is a SHA-256 commitment to the entire prior ledger. The customer signed this commitment with their own private key.

If the vendor subsequently alters any manufacturing entry, the altered record will not match the `verified_chain_hash` recorded in the customer's independently held RECEIPT entry. Since the customer signed the RECEIPT entry, the customer can prove to any third party — including a court — that the vendor's current records do not match what the customer verified at receipt.

The vendor cannot:
- Alter any manufacturing entry after customer receipt without creating a detectable hash mismatch
- Revoke the vendor signing key in a way that retroactively invalidates customer-held signed records (the customer holds the signed data itself, not merely a reference to vendor-held records)
- Deny any event in the ledger, because the customer holds vendor-signed cryptographic proof of each event independent of any vendor system

**2.3 Acquisition Context — Government Procurement**

This vendor-irrevocability mechanism is particularly valuable in government procurement contexts. A federal agency procuring hardware attestation devices (such as ZKKey or PowerVerify under ZKNOT's product line) receives, at time of delivery acceptance, a cryptographic record of the entire manufacturing and configuration history of each device. The agency does not depend on ZKNOT's continued cooperation or record-keeping to prove what was in the device at delivery. The signed RECEIPT entry serves as an independently auditable delivery acceptance record.

This addresses a specific gap in current federal acquisition practice: FISMA, CMMC, and NIST SP 800-161 supply chain risk management requirements do not specify how a procuring agency can independently verify hardware provenance without ongoing vendor dependency.

### 3. Lifecycle Extension

After customer receipt, the ledger is extensible with post-receipt lifecycle events:
- `FIRMWARE_UPDATE`: new firmware version, update authorization, updating party signature
- `RECALIBRATION`: updated calibration parameters, recalibration party signature
- `REDEPLOYMENT`: transfer to new operational context, receiving party signature
- `FIELD_EVENT`: operator-recorded field events with device attestation
- `DECOMMISSION`: end-of-life recording, destroying party signature

Each lifecycle event is signed by the party responsible and appended to the chain, maintaining continuous hash-linked history from genesis key generation through decommission.

### 4. Integration with ZK-LocalChain

In a preferred embodiment, the device manufacturing ledger is compatible with the ZK-LocalChain event ledger format (PAT-004). Field events recorded during device operation — attestation artifacts generated by ZKKey or PowerVerify — can be recorded in the same chain as manufacturing events, creating a single continuous ledger from silicon provisioning through field operation. This creates an unbroken cryptographic chain from device birth to any specific field attestation event.

This integration claim — manufacturing ledger and field operation ledger as a single continuous hash chain — is a novel combination not anticipated by either the device provisioning art or the operational attestation art independently.

### 5. Verification Protocol

**5.1 Full Lifecycle Verification:**
1. Load complete ledger from device or customer backup
2. Verify genesis entry (device SE self-signature)
3. Verify each vendor-signed entry against vendor public key
4. Verify hash chain continuity (no gaps, no broken links)
5. Verify customer RECEIPT entry signature
6. Verify all post-receipt lifecycle entries against respective signing parties
7. Confirm no gaps in event sequence

**5.2 Partial Verification:** Any party with access to the relevant public keys can verify any subset of the ledger independently. An auditor can verify manufacturing entries without customer credentials. A court can verify the complete chain using only standard ECDSA verification tools.

## CLAIMS

**Claim 1.** A device attestation system comprising:
a manufacturing event recorder configured to capture device manufacturing events and record each event as a signed hash-chained ledger entry;
a device-resident ledger stored in non-volatile storage accessible to the device, the ledger containing all manufacturing event records;
a customer receipt mechanism configured to: read the device-resident ledger at customer receipt; verify vendor signatures and hash chain continuity; create a customer-signed receipt entry containing a commitment hash of all prior vendor-signed entries; and store an independent copy of the ledger in customer-controlled storage;
wherein after the customer receipt mechanism executes, the vendor cannot alter, revoke, or deny any recorded manufacturing event without creating a detectable mismatch with the customer-held independently signed ledger.

**Claim 2.** The system of claim 1, wherein manufacturing event records are hash-chained such that each entry contains the SHA-256 hash of the immediately preceding entry, and wherein insertion, deletion, or modification of any entry is detectable by recomputing the hash chain.

**Claim 3.** The system of claim 1, wherein the first entry in the device-resident ledger is a key generation event signed by the device's hardware secure element, establishing on-device key generation without vendor key import.

**Claim 4.** The system of claim 1, wherein the customer receipt entry comprises a verified chain hash field containing the SHA-256 hash of the complete vendor-signed ledger at time of receipt, and wherein the customer receipt entry is signed by a customer-held private key.

**Claim 5.** The system of claim 1, wherein the device-resident ledger is extensible with post-receipt lifecycle events signed by authorized parties, each event hash-chained to the preceding entry.

**Claim 6.** The system of claim 5, wherein post-receipt lifecycle events include field operation events produced by the device's hardware secure element during operational deployment, creating a continuous chain from manufacturing through field operation.

**Claim 7.** The system of claim 1, wherein the device-resident ledger is compatible with the ZK-LocalChain event ledger format, enabling manufacturing events and field attestation events to be recorded in a single continuous hash chain.

**Claim 8.** A method for vendor-irrevocable device attestation comprising:
recording device manufacturing events as signed hash-chained ledger entries in a device-resident ledger;
at device receipt, having the customer verify all vendor signatures and hash chain continuity;
having the customer create a receipt entry signed by a customer-held private key, the receipt entry containing a commitment hash of all prior vendor-signed entries;
storing an independent copy of the ledger including the customer receipt entry in customer-controlled storage;
wherein the commitment hash in the signed receipt entry makes any subsequent vendor alteration of manufacturing records detectable by comparison with the customer-held record.

**Claim 9.** The method of claim 8, further comprising verifying at any time that the vendor's current manufacturing records match the commitment hash in the customer-held receipt entry.

**Claim 10.** The method of claim 8, further comprising appending post-receipt lifecycle events to the ledger, each event signed by the responsible party and hash-chained to the preceding entry.

**Claim 11.** A device-resident manufacturing ledger comprising:
a genesis entry signed by a device hardware secure element certifying on-device key generation;
a plurality of vendor-signed manufacturing entries, each containing a hash of the preceding entry;
a customer receipt entry signed by a customer-held key, containing a commitment hash of all prior vendor-signed entries;
wherein the customer receipt entry makes the vendor-signed manufacturing entries vendor-irrevocable after customer receipt.

**Claim 12.** The device-resident manufacturing ledger of claim 11, wherein the ledger is stored in tamper-resistant hardware of the device, and wherein reading the ledger requires no network connectivity and no vendor system access.

## ABSTRACT

A vendor-irrevocable device attestation system records hardware manufacturing events — key generation, firmware loading, calibration, inspection, and shipment — as signed, hash-chained entries in a device-resident ledger. At customer receipt, a verification mechanism reads the complete ledger, verifies all vendor signatures and hash chain continuity, and creates a customer-signed receipt entry containing a cryptographic commitment to the entire prior vendor-signed record. An independent copy of the ledger is stored in customer-controlled storage. After this receipt event, no subsequent vendor action can alter, revoke, or deny any manufacturing record without creating a detectable mismatch with the customer's independently held signed copy. The system extends to include post-receipt lifecycle events and field operation events in a single continuous hash chain from device genesis through operational decommission.

## COMMERCIAL SIGNIFICANCE — AXON ACQUISITION CONTEXT

This patent directly addresses Axon's supply chain integrity problem. Axon deploys hardware devices (body cameras, Tasers, evidence handling equipment) to thousands of law enforcement agencies. Those agencies currently cannot independently verify device provenance without trusting Axon. PAT-005 gives Axon a product capability: every device shipped carries a vendor-irrevocable manufacturing ledger that the customer agency holds independently. This converts device provenance from a trust question to a verification question — consistent with ZKNOT's core doctrine.

This is a government procurement differentiator for direct ZKNOT sales (law enforcement agencies can verify ZKKey and PowerVerify provenance without trusting ZKNOT), and a product feature for any Axon acquisition (Axon can offer the same capability for its own devices).

CFO Suggestions — Longevity & Strategic Resilience
① Build a SaaS Recurring Revenue Base — Even One Subscriber Rewrites the Story
ZK-LocalChain at $49/month is 99%+ margin and creates ARR. A single subscriber before G8 lets you present an ARR trajectory rather than a revenue snapshot. At G8, $49/mo × 12 = $588 ARR at 10x multiple = $5,880 valuation contribution — from one customer. Ten subscribers = $58,800 added to your data room valuation at near-zero cost. This is the most capital-efficient revenue path available.
Why it matters for longevity: If acquisition doesn't close at G8, SaaS revenue sustains the company indefinitely at low burn. Hardware revenue is one-time. SaaS is compounding.
② Don't Build for One Acquirer — Cultivate Three
Axon, Cellebrite, and Motorola Solutions are named targets. The CFO recommends identifying and making informal contact with at least 2 additional strategic acquirers (e.g., Axon's competitors, federal forensics software companies, or defense prime contractors with digital evidence gaps) before G8. A competitive acquisition process — even a perceived one — is worth $2–5M in negotiating leverage on the final price.
Why it matters for longevity: A single-acquirer strategy is a single point of failure. If that acquirer walks, the company needs runway to find another. Multiple warm relationships eliminate that cliff.
③ File the SBIR Application the Moment Reauthorization Passes
The budget template exists (Section 5.2 of CFO-001). The narrative exists. When reauthorization passes, a 72-hour submission window is realistic only if the application shell is pre-written. CFO recommends drafting the full Phase I application narrative now — agency problem statement, technical approach, commercialization plan — so submission is a copy-paste + sign operation, not a writing sprint.
Why it matters for longevity: $150–275K non-dilutive capital extends runway by 12–18 months and adds a federal validation stamp that standalone revenue cannot replicate. It also makes the company viable as a standalone if acquisition timing slips.
④ Negotiate a Fixed-Fee Arrangement with the Patent Attorney Now
The current model shows ~$9,500–12,000 in attorney fees with no fixed-fee agreements in place. Hourly billing on patent prosecution is unpredictable and the single largest budget risk. Before PAT-001 non-provisional work begins, negotiate a flat fee per non-provisional (target: $2,800–3,200 per filing for a straightforward hardware patent). Bundle PAT-001 and PAT-007 for a package discount.
Why it matters for longevity: Uncapped attorney fees are the fastest path to the Danger Case scenario. A $500 attorney overage per filing across 4 filings = $2,000 gone from the reserve. Fixed fees convert the largest budget risk into a known cost.
⑤ Create a Minimal Viable Standalone Plan for If G8 Slips
The current plan assumes acquisition by or shortly after September 18, 2026. If the right acquirer isn't in the room by then, the company needs a Plan B that doesn't require additional capital raises. That plan exists in rough form in the projections (standalone 2027: $100–300K). CFO recommends formalizing it: what does ZKNOT look like as a $200K/year bootstrapped forensics tool vendor? That answer makes the CEO a better negotiator — acquirers can tell when a founder has no alternative.
Why it matters for longevity: The best acquisition negotiation position is not needing the acquisition. A credible standalone path is leverage, not failure.
⑥ Start the R&D Tax Credit Clock — Document Every Technical Hour Now
Section 41 R&D credits apply to firmware development, cryptographic protocol work, and hardware testing — all of which are actively happening. Even pre-revenue, these credits accumulate and can either offset future tax liability or transfer to an acquirer as part of deal structuring. The cost of documentation is zero. The cost of not documenting is the entire credit.
Why it matters for longevity: Documented R&D credits are a data room asset. They signal disciplined financial management and may reduce the acquirer's effective cost of the deal.

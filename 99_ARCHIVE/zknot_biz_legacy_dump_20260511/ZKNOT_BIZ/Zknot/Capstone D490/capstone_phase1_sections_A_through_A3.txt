SECTION A: SECURITY PROBLEM UNDER INVESTIGATION
===============================================================================

The security problem under investigation is the inability of current logging and audit systems to maintain cryptographic integrity during periods of network unavailability. Organizations that rely on digital event logging for security monitoring, forensic investigation, and compliance auditing face a fundamental weakness: when network connectivity is lost, their logging systems either stop recording events entirely (creating gaps in the audit trail) or buffer events locally without cryptographic protection, making those buffered logs vulnerable to tampering before they can be transmitted to a central server.

This problem becomes critical in environments where network connectivity cannot be guaranteed. Rural law enforcement agencies operate in areas with inconsistent cellular coverage. Mobile investigation units move between locations and frequently lose connectivity. International operations encounter unreliable internet infrastructure across different jurisdictions. Corporate compliance systems in network-restricted facilities may not have continuous access to external logging services. In all of these scenarios, the same fundamental issue arises: events must be logged locally during disconnection periods, but there is no standardized mechanism to ensure those locally-stored logs maintain cryptographic integrity until synchronization can occur.

The second dimension of this problem emerges when multiple devices are logging events independently during the same disconnection period. When these devices eventually reconnect and need to synchronize their event chains, there is no established protocol for merging those chains while detecting tampering, resolving conflicts, and maintaining a verifiable timeline of what actually occurred. Current centralized logging systems assume a hub-and-spoke model where all logs flow to a single aggregation point. They are not designed to handle the scenario where multiple independent sources have been generating events concurrently and now need to establish a consistent, cryptographically verifiable view of what happened across all devices.

This creates a cybersecurity gap where organizations cannot reliably prove the integrity of event logs collected during network disruption. In forensic investigations, legal proceedings, and compliance audits, the evidentiary value of these logs is undermined because there is no way to cryptographically demonstrate that the logs were not altered between the time events occurred and the time they were transmitted to the central server.

SECTION A1: IMPORTANCE, BACKGROUND, AND ENVIRONMENT
===============================================================================

IMPORTANCE OF THE PROBLEM

The integrity of digital event logs underpins modern cybersecurity operations, forensic investigations, and regulatory compliance efforts. When security incidents occur, investigators rely on audit logs to reconstruct what happened, identify the scope of compromise, and determine appropriate remediation actions. When regulatory auditors review an organization's controls, they examine logs to verify that policies were followed and that unauthorized actions were detected. When legal proceedings require digital evidence, logs serve as the foundation for establishing timelines and proving facts.

However, the evidentiary value of logs depends entirely on their integrity. A log that can be silently altered has no forensic value. An audit trail with gaps cannot prove compliance. A timeline that can be rewritten cannot be trusted in legal proceedings. This is why cryptographic integrity—the ability to prove that logs have not been tampered with—is not just a technical nicety but a fundamental requirement for any system that relies on logs as evidence.

The problem becomes acute when we consider that many organizations now operate in environments where continuous network connectivity cannot be assumed. The shift toward mobile operations, field investigations, and distributed workforces means that evidence collection increasingly happens outside the traditional office network. A rural sheriff's department collecting evidence at a remote crime scene cannot wait until they return to the station to start logging their actions. A mobile forensics team investigating a corporate incident needs to document their findings as they occur, not after they find reliable WiFi. An international investigation involving multiple jurisdictions cannot be paralyzed by intermittent internet access.


BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

Current centralized logging architectures were designed for an environment where endpoint devices had reliable, continuous network access to a central log aggregation server. In this model, logs are generated on endpoints (workstations, servers, network devices) and immediately transmitted via syslog, SIEM agents, or proprietary protocols to a central server for storage, indexing, and analysis. This centralization provides operational benefits: logs are aggregated in one place for analysis, retention policies can be enforced centrally, and security teams have a unified view across the organization.

However, this architecture has a critical assumption baked in: network connectivity is available when events need to be logged. When that assumption fails, the architecture breaks down. Most logging systems handle network disruption in one of two ways:

1. Stop logging entirely. If the device cannot reach the log server, it simply discards events or stops generating logs. This creates gaps in the audit trail—periods where no one can prove what did or did not happen.

2. Buffer logs locally without protection. The device stores logs in local memory or disk, hoping to transmit them later when connectivity returns. But during the buffering period, those logs have no cryptographic protection. An attacker who compromises the device can modify, delete, or fabricate log entries before they're ever transmitted to the central server.

Neither approach is acceptable for high-integrity scenarios like forensic investigations or compliance audits. You cannot prove what happened during a network outage if you have no logs. You cannot trust logs that were buffered without cryptographic protection.

Recent developments in distributed systems research have explored cryptographic approaches to tamper-evident logging, including hash chains (Schneier & Kelsey, 1999), certificate transparency logs (Laurie et al., 2013), and blockchain-based audit trails (Nakamoto, 2008). These approaches demonstrate that cryptographic techniques can detect tampering even in adversarial environments. However, existing implementations assume either continuous network connectivity (certificate transparency logs require access to log servers) or distributed consensus (blockchains require ongoing participation in a consensus protocol). Neither approach works well for devices that need to operate independently during extended network outages and then synchronize later.

Additionally, when multiple devices have been operating independently, existing systems struggle with conflict resolution. Git and other distributed version control systems provide inspiration for how to merge divergent histories, but they are designed for source code with manual conflict resolution, not for automated event logging where conflicts need to be resolved deterministically based on cryptographic evidence.


ENVIRONMENT WHERE THE PROBLEM EXISTS

This problem manifests in several distinct operational environments:

1. FIELD INVESTIGATIONS AND MOBILE OPERATIONS
Law enforcement agencies, corporate investigators, and digital forensics teams frequently operate in environments where network connectivity is unreliable or unavailable. A detective collecting evidence at a rural crime scene may have no cellular coverage. A forensics examiner investigating an incident at a remote facility may be working in an air-gapped environment. A mobile investigation unit traveling between locations loses connectivity during transit.

In these scenarios, investigators need to maintain detailed audit logs of their actions (what evidence was collected, when it was photographed, who handled it) to establish chain-of-custody and meet evidentiary standards. Current practice relies on manual logbooks or unprotected digital notes, neither of which provides cryptographic proof of integrity.

2. INTERNATIONAL AND MULTI-JURISDICTIONAL OPERATIONS
Investigations that span multiple countries or jurisdictions encounter vastly different network infrastructure quality. An investigator working in a major metropolitan area may have excellent connectivity, while a colleague working in a developing region may have intermittent or unreliable internet access. When these teams need to combine their findings into a unified timeline, there is no established mechanism to merge their independently-collected event logs while proving that neither set has been tampered with.

3. COMPLIANCE AND AUDIT IN RESTRICTED ENVIRONMENTS
Some organizations operate facilities where network access is deliberately restricted for security or regulatory reasons. Manufacturing facilities handling classified information, research labs working with export-controlled technology, or financial institutions processing sensitive transactions may have network segmentation policies that prevent certain systems from accessing external logging services. These environments still require comprehensive audit logs for compliance, but the logs must be collected locally and synchronized only during approved windows when network access is permitted.

4. RESOURCE-CONSTRAINED AND EMBEDDED SYSTEMS
Internet of Things (IoT) devices, embedded systems, and edge computing nodes often operate with intermittent connectivity due to power constraints, bandwidth limitations, or network reliability issues. These devices may need to log security-relevant events (configuration changes, access attempts, sensor readings) but cannot maintain continuous connections to central logging infrastructure.


CURRENT STATE OF THE PROBLEM

At present, organizations working in these environments face an uncomfortable choice:

Option 1: Accept gaps in the audit trail. When network access is unavailable, stop logging or rely on manual records. This makes forensic reconstruction difficult or impossible and may violate compliance requirements that mandate continuous audit trails.

Option 2: Buffer logs locally without cryptographic protection and hope they are not tampered with before synchronization. This creates a window of vulnerability where an attacker who compromises a device can rewrite history by altering buffered logs.

Option 3: Deploy specialized centralized infrastructure at every location where logging might occur. This is prohibitively expensive and still doesn't solve the problem for truly mobile operations or unpredictable field locations.

None of these options provides a satisfactory solution. The fundamental gap is the lack of a protocol that:
- Maintains cryptographic integrity during network outages
- Allows devices to operate independently and then synchronize later  
- Detects tampering even if logs were buffered for extended periods
- Resolves conflicts when multiple devices recorded events concurrently
- Works without requiring continuous network connectivity or distributed consensus


PROPOSED SOLUTION ENVIRONMENT

This capstone project addresses the problem by implementing a synchronization protocol for cryptographically chained event ledgers. The solution will be demonstrated using prototype hardware components from the ZKNOT.IO evidence integrity platform:

- PowerVerify™ devices for power-only interfaces (preventing data exfiltration during evidence collection)
- ZKKey™ human-gated signing devices (requiring physical button press for cryptographic signatures)
- Embedded Linux systems with ATECC608 secure element hardware for tamper-resistant key storage

The hardware is currently in production at JLCPCB (a professional PCB manufacturer), with delivery expected after the Chinese New Year holiday (Q1 2026). Until hardware arrival, the protocol will be implemented and tested in a simulated environment using software-based cryptographic operations.

The target deployment environment for this solution includes:
- Law enforcement evidence collection in rural or remote areas
- Corporate forensics investigations in network-restricted facilities
- International operations requiring multi-jurisdictional evidence coordination
- Compliance logging systems that must maintain audit trails during network disruption
- Any scenario where cryptographic proof of log integrity is required but continuous network connectivity cannot be guaranteed

SECTION A2: DOCUMENTATION DEMONSTRATING NEED FOR SOLUTION
===============================================================================

The need for cryptographic event synchronization protocols in intermittent-connectivity environments is documented in academic research, industry white papers, and regulatory guidance:

ACADEMIC RESEARCH ON TAMPER-EVIDENT LOGGING

Schneier and Kelsey's seminal 1999 paper "Secure Audit Logs to Support Computer Forensics" (ACM Transactions on Information and System Security, 2(2), 159-176) established the foundational requirements for cryptographically protected audit logs. They demonstrated that traditional logging systems are vulnerable to tampering and proposed forward-secure cryptographic mechanisms to detect modification. However, their approach assumes a central trusted server is available for log storage. The paper explicitly states: "We assume that the attacker cannot prevent the delivery of log entries to the logging machine." This assumption fails in intermittent-connectivity scenarios.

Crosby and Wallach (2009) extended this work in "Efficient Data Structures for Tamper-Evident Logging" (USENIX Security Symposium), introducing Merkle tree-based structures for efficient verification of large log sets. Their work demonstrates the cryptographic feasibility of tamper detection but still assumes centralized architecture. They note: "The main limitation of our system is that it requires trust in the logging server." Again, this approach does not address distributed scenarios where devices must operate independently.

Lamport's classic 1978 paper "Time, Clocks, and the Ordering of Events in a Distributed System" (Communications of the ACM, 21(7), 558-565) provides the theoretical foundation for establishing event ordering in distributed systems without synchronized clocks. His work on logical clocks and happened-before relationships is directly applicable to the conflict resolution problem when merging event chains from devices that have operated concurrently. However, Lamport's work predates modern cryptographic techniques and does not address tamper-evidence requirements.

Fidge (1988) extended Lamport's work with vector clocks in "Timestamps in Message-Passing Systems that Preserve the Partial Ordering" (Australian Computer Science Communications), providing a mechanism for concurrent event ordering that preserves causality. This mechanism is essential for resolving conflicts in our synchronization protocol.


INDUSTRY GUIDANCE AND WHITE PAPERS

The NIST Special Publication 800-92 "Guide to Computer Security Log Management" (2006) provides guidance on log management for federal agencies and establishes requirements for log integrity. Section 3.1.2 explicitly identifies "log confidentiality and integrity" as a fundamental requirement, stating: "Logs can be modified or destroyed by attackers to cover up malicious activity or to otherwise hamper log analysis." The document recommends cryptographic mechanisms but does not provide specific protocols for intermittent-connectivity scenarios.

NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5 "Security and Privacy Controls for Information Systems and Organizations" includes control AU-9 "Protection of Audit Information" which requires organizations to "protect audit information and audit logging tools from unauthorized access, modification, and deletion." Organizations operating in environments with unreliable network connectivity currently struggle to meet this control because there is no established mechanism to cryptographically protect logs during buffering periods.

The Cloud Security Alliance's "Security Guidance for Critical Areas of Cloud Computing" warns about the risks of centralized logging dependencies: "Organizations must ensure that audit logs maintain integrity even when connectivity to central logging infrastructure is disrupted." However, the guidance does not provide specific technical solutions for this scenario.


REGULATORY AND COMPLIANCE DRIVERS

Multiple regulatory frameworks create explicit requirements for tamper-evident audit logs:

HIPAA Security Rule (45 CFR §164.312(b)): Healthcare organizations must implement "hardware, software, and/or procedural mechanisms that record and examine activity in information systems that contain or use electronic protected health information." The regulation requires that audit logs be protected from alteration, but many covered entities struggle to maintain this protection during network outages in mobile healthcare settings (ambulances, home healthcare, rural clinics).

PCI-DSS Requirement 10: Organizations handling payment card data must "track and monitor all access to network resources and cardholder data." Specifically, 10.3.4 requires that "logs cannot be altered." Organizations with mobile point-of-sale systems or retail locations in areas with poor connectivity currently cannot fully meet this requirement because their logs lack cryptographic protection during buffering periods.

SOX Section 404: Public companies must maintain comprehensive audit trails for financial systems. The PCAOB auditing standards require that these logs be tamper-evident. Companies with international operations or distributed financial systems face challenges meeting this requirement when network connectivity is unreliable.

GDPR Article 32: Requires "the ability to ensure the ongoing confidentiality, integrity, availability and resilience of processing systems and services." For organizations processing personal data in mobile or field environments, ensuring log integrity during network disruption is a compliance gap.


FORENSIC INVESTIGATION STANDARDS

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) published SP 800-86 "Guide to Integrating Forensic Techniques into Incident Response" which emphasizes the importance of establishing chain-of-custody for digital evidence. The document states: "To be admissible in court, evidence must be relevant, authentic, accurate, complete, and convincing." Authenticity requires proving that evidence has not been altered since collection.

The Digital Forensics Research Workshop (DFRWS) technical working group has identified "evidence integrity in mobile and field environments" as a critical research gap. Their 2023 roadmap specifically calls for "cryptographic protocols that can establish chain-of-custody for evidence collected in environments with intermittent network access."


INDUSTRY INCIDENTS DEMONSTRATING THE PROBLEM

While specific incident reports often remain confidential, industry surveys document the scale of the problem:

The 2024 Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report found that in 37% of investigated incidents, attackers had modified or deleted audit logs to cover their tracks. The report notes: "Organizations with centralized logging detected log manipulation faster than those relying on local logging, but organizations in the 'local logging' category often had no reliable network connectivity available."

The SANS Institute's 2023 survey "Security Logging and Monitoring" found that 62% of organizations reported gaps in their audit logs due to network connectivity issues, and 44% had no mechanism to cryptographically verify the integrity of logs collected during network outages.


TECHNICAL EVIDENCE: CERTIFICATE TRANSPARENCY AS A MODEL

The Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) RFC 6962 "Certificate Transparency" (Laurie, Langley, & Kasper, 2013) demonstrates a production-scale deployment of cryptographically verifiable, append-only logs. Certificate Transparency uses Merkle trees to create tamper-evident logs of SSL/TLS certificates, allowing anyone to verify that certificates have not been fraudulently issued. This system has been successfully deployed by major certificate authorities and browser vendors.

However, Certificate Transparency assumes continuous network connectivity. Log servers must be online and accessible for certificates to be logged and verified. There is no mechanism for operating during network outages and synchronizing later. Our capstone project extends these principles to intermittent-connectivity scenarios.


SUMMARY OF DOCUMENTED NEED

The academic literature, industry guidance, regulatory requirements, and forensic standards all converge on the same gap: there is no established protocol for maintaining cryptographically verifiable event logs in environments with intermittent network connectivity and then synchronizing those logs from multiple independent sources. This gap creates compliance risks, forensic limitations, and operational challenges for organizations working in mobile, field, or distributed environments.

Our capstone project directly addresses this documented need by implementing a synchronization protocol that:
- Uses cryptographic hash chains to create tamper-evident logs (building on Schneier & Kelsey)
- Employs vector clocks or Lamport timestamps for conflict resolution (building on Lamport & Fidge)
- Operates during network outages without requiring continuous connectivity
- Synchronizes and merges event chains from multiple devices
- Detects tampering, deletion, or reordering of events through cryptographic verification

SECTION A3: ROOT CAUSES OF THE PROBLEM
===============================================================================

The inability of current logging systems to maintain cryptographic integrity during network disruption stems from several interconnected root causes:


ROOT CAUSE 1: ARCHITECTURAL ASSUMPTION OF CONTINUOUS CONNECTIVITY

Modern centralized logging systems (Splunk, Elastic Stack, AWS CloudWatch, Azure Monitor) were architected during an era when enterprise systems operated primarily within corporate networks with reliable connectivity. The fundamental design pattern is:

1. Generate log event on endpoint
2. Immediately transmit to central server
3. Central server stores, indexes, and protects logs

This architecture provides operational benefits (centralized management, unified analysis, enforced retention), but it embeds a critical assumption: steps 2 and 3 can occur immediately after step 1. When this assumption fails due to network disruption, the architecture breaks down.

EVIDENCE: Architecture documentation for major SIEM platforms consistently describes network connectivity as a fundamental requirement. Splunk's deployment documentation states: "Forwarders require network connectivity to the indexer." Elastic Stack documentation notes: "Beats agents send data to Elasticsearch or Logstash over the network." These systems have no native capability to maintain cryptographic integrity during buffering.

The root cause is not that these systems made a poor engineering choice at the time they were designed. Rather, the operational environment has evolved. Mobile operations, field investigations, and distributed workforces now require logging in scenarios that violate the continuous connectivity assumption. The systems have not evolved to match this changed environment.


ROOT CAUSE 2: LACK OF STANDARDIZED CRYPTOGRAPHIC PROTECTION DURING BUFFERING

When network connectivity is lost, logging systems must buffer events locally. However, there is no widely adopted standard for how to cryptographically protect these buffered events. Different vendors implement different approaches:

- Some systems buffer in plaintext files with no integrity protection
- Some use checksums (which can be recomputed by an attacker who modifies the log)
- Some encrypt buffered logs (which provides confidentiality but not tamper-evidence)
- Some use proprietary integrity mechanisms that cannot be independently verified

The absence of a standardized cryptographic buffering protocol means that organizations cannot reliably prove their buffered logs have not been tampered with.

EVIDENCE: A survey of logging agent implementations (syslog-ng, rsyslog, Fluentd, Filebeat) shows that none implements cryptographic hash chaining or digital signatures for locally buffered logs. They rely on file system permissions and encryption-at-rest, both of which can be circumvented by an attacker with administrative access.

The root cause is that buffering was originally conceived as a temporary measure to handle brief network hiccups (seconds or minutes), not extended outages (hours or days). As a result, the buffering mechanism was not designed with the same integrity guarantees as the centralized storage.


ROOT CAUSE 3: ABSENCE OF DISTRIBUTED SYNCHRONIZATION PROTOCOLS FOR EVENT LOGS

When multiple devices have been logging events independently, they need to synchronize their event chains into a consistent view. However, distributed systems protocols (Raft, Paxos, Byzantine fault tolerance) are designed for scenarios where nodes can communicate during the consensus process. They are not designed for the scenario where devices have been completely disconnected and are only now attempting to reconcile what happened.

Git and other distributed version control systems provide inspiration for merging divergent histories, but they are designed for source code where:
- A human can manually resolve conflicts ("did I want version A or version B of this function?")
- It's acceptable to pause and ask the user to make a decision
- The merge process is collaborative and iterative

Event logs have different requirements:
- Conflict resolution must be deterministic (no human can decide "which of these two events really happened first?")
- The merge must be automated (no user interaction)
- Both event chains must be preserved in their entirety (you cannot discard events just because they conflict)

The root cause is that existing distributed synchronization protocols assume either (a) nodes can communicate during the process, or (b) humans can manually resolve conflicts. Neither assumption holds for automated event log synchronization in intermittent-connectivity scenarios.

EVIDENCE: Lamport's 1978 work on logical clocks provides a theoretical foundation for ordering events in distributed systems, but his work predates modern cryptographic techniques and does not address tamper-evidence. More recent work on distributed databases (DynamoDB, Cassandra) uses vector clocks for conflict resolution, but these systems assume eventual connectivity and do not provide cryptographic proof of event integrity. There is no existing system that combines cryptographic hash chains with distributed conflict resolution for event logs.


ROOT CAUSE 4: CONFLATION OF LOGGING AND REAL-TIME ALERTING

Many modern logging systems conflate two distinct functions: event recording (preserving a record of what happened) and real-time alerting (notifying analysts about suspicious activity). This conflation drives architectural decisions that prioritize low-latency transmission to the central server, because alerts need to be real-time.

However, not all logging scenarios require real-time alerting. Forensic investigations work with historical logs collected after an incident. Compliance audits examine logs from past periods. Chain-of-custody documentation is reviewed only when evidence goes to trial. In these scenarios, it's acceptable for logs to be synchronized hours or days after events occur, as long as the logs can be cryptographically proven to be intact and unmodified.

By conflating recording and alerting, current systems make it difficult to deploy logging in scenarios where real-time transmission is not possible but delayed synchronization with cryptographic integrity would be acceptable.

EVIDENCE: SIEM vendor documentation consistently positions real-time alerting as a core value proposition. Splunk Enterprise Security promises "real-time threat detection." IBM QRadar advertises "real-time analytics." This emphasis on real-time processing drives architectural decisions that assume continuous connectivity. There is little focus on scenarios where delayed synchronization is acceptable.


ROOT CAUSE 5: LACK OF HARDWARE-BASED TRUST ANCHORS IN STANDARD LOGGING DEPLOYMENTS

Cryptographic integrity depends on protecting the signing keys used to authenticate logs. If an attacker can steal the key, they can sign fabricated logs that will pass cryptographic verification. Current logging systems typically store keys in software (configuration files, operating system keystores) where they can be extracted by an attacker with administrative access.

Hardware security modules (HSMs) and secure elements (like the ATECC608) provide tamper-resistant key storage, but they are not typically integrated into standard logging deployments. They are seen as expensive, specialized components reserved for high-security scenarios like certificate authorities or payment processing.

The root cause is that logging has historically been viewed as an operational function (system administrators setting up syslog) rather than a security function requiring cryptographic key management. As logging has become more central to security operations and forensic investigations, the infrastructure has not evolved to include hardware-based trust anchors.

EVIDENCE: Deployment guides for standard logging agents (rsyslog, syslog-ng, Fluentd) do not mention hardware security modules or secure elements. Keys for TLS connections are stored in PEM files on the file system. An attacker who compromises the system can extract these keys and forge logs.


SUMMARY OF ROOT CAUSES AND PATH TO SOLUTION

The root causes converge on a fundamental mismatch:

Current logging architectures assume:
- Continuous network connectivity
- Centralized trust and storage
- Real-time transmission
- Software-based key management

Many operational environments require:
- Operation during network disruption
- Distributed collection and delayed synchronization
- Cryptographic proof of integrity
- Hardware-based key protection

Our capstone project addresses these root causes by implementing a protocol that:
- Uses cryptographic hash chains to protect events during buffering (addressing Root Cause 2)
- Synchronizes event chains using vector clocks or Lamport timestamps (addressing Root Cause 3)
- Separates event recording from real-time alerting, allowing delayed synchronization (addressing Root Cause 4)
- Integrates hardware security elements (ATECC608) for tamper-resistant key storage (addressing Root Cause 5)
- Explicitly designs for intermittent connectivity rather than assuming continuous network access (addressing Root Cause 1)

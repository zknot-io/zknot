## PHILOSOPHICAL PILLARS

### 1. Physics > Software
**"Physics is a better determiner of security than software."**

- Software has bugs, backdoors, updates
- Physics has laws that don't change
- Software can be compromised
- Physics can only be what it is

**Implication:** Build security through physical constraint, not code complexity.

---

### 2. Math > Features
**"Math is a better determiner of safety than features."**

- Features expand attack surface
- Math reduces state space
- Features require trust in implementation
- Math requires only verification

**Implication:** Cryptographic proof over functional promises.

---

### 3. Verification > Trust
**"When physics is policy, trust is optional."**

- Trust requires belief
- Verification requires inspection
- Trust degrades over time
- Verification remains constant

**Implication:** Build systems where trust is unnecessary, not just "trustworthy."

## THE THREE-LAYER SYSTEM

### Layer 1: Physical Constraint
**Physics enforces.**

The hardware makes certain states impossible.
Not through policy. Through physical law.

**Example:** PowerVerify has no data signaling primitives.
Data transfer isn't blocked—it's **impossible by construction**.

### Layer 2: Cryptographic Attestation
**Math proves.**

The system creates unforgeable records of what occurred.
Not through logging. Through mathematics.

**Example:** ZKey creates cryptographic attestations.
Events aren't reported—they're **mathematically bound to reality**.

### Layer 3: Independent Verification
**You verify.**

Anyone can inspect the constraint and validate the proof.
Not through audit reports. Through direct verification.

**Example:** verifyknot.io enables third-party validation.
Trust isn't required—**verification is possible**.

## LANGUAGE STANDARDS

### What We Say

✅ **"Physically constrained"**
✅ **"Data transfer impossible by construction"**
✅ **"Designed for verification"**
✅ **"Cryptographically attested"**
✅ **"Admissible evidence"**
✅ **"Verify our constraints"**
✅ **"Third-party verifiable"**
✅ **"Electrically incapable of data exchange"**

### What We Never Say

❌ **"Secure"** (vague, overused, implies trust)
❌ **"Safe"** (subjective, liability-laden)
❌ **"Trust us"** (defeats the entire thesis)
❌ **"Guaranteed"** (legal exposure)
❌ **"Protected"** (passive, unclear)
❌ **"Certified"** (unless literally certified)
❌ **"Nothing malicious"** (unprovable negative)

### Constraint Language (Use This)

When describing what devices **don't do**, use:

- **"Impossible by construction"**
- **"No mechanism exists for X"**
- **"Physically incapable of Y"**
- **"Lacks the electrical primitives required for Z"**
- **"Constrained to single-purpose operation"**

These statements are:
- Falsifiable
- Inspectable
- Legally defensible
- Survive cross-examination

## MARKETING PRINCIPLES

### 1. Lead with Constraint, Not Capability

❌ **"PowerVerify keeps your data safe"**
✅ **"PowerVerify makes data transfer impossible"**

The second is stronger because it's verifiable.

### 2. Proof Over Promises

❌ **"We promise to protect you"**
✅ **"We provide proof that only X occurred"**

Promises require trust. Proof requires verification.

### 3. Negative Space is Your Strength

❌ **"Feature-rich security device"**
✅ **"Single-purpose constraint, verifiable by design"**

Fewer capabilities = smaller attack surface = stronger evidence.

### 4. Let Others Vouch, Never Self-Assert

❌ **"ZKNOT is the most secure solution"**
✅ **"Verify ZKNOT's constraints yourself at verifyknot.io"**

The strongest claim is no claim—just an invitation to verify.

## WHEN IN DOUBT

If the answer is "trust us," rewrite it.

**"Would this statement survive cross-examination?"**

**"Are we claiming capability or stating constraint?"**
